SOCIAL ENGINEERING

Social Engineering is a technique commonly used by cybercriminals and often used in conjunction with other network attacks;
it is used to deceive unsuspecting targets to reveal confidential information. It could be said that it is an act of manipulating an
individual into performing actions or divulging confidential information. It also often relies upon human nature and people's willingness to be helpful.





SOCIAL ENGINEERING TECHNIQUES

PRETEXTING - When an attacker pretends to need a personal data to confirm the identity of the recipient.
SPEAR PHISHING - When an attacker creates a targeted phishing attack on a specific individual or organization.
SPAM - Unsolicited emails that often contain links that are harmful, malware, deceptive content.
SOMETHING FOR SOMETHING - When an attacker request personal information in exchange for something.
BAITING - When an attacker leaves a malware infected flash drive in public places.
IMPERSONATION - When an attacker pretends to be someone they are not.
TAILGATING - When an attacker follows an authorized person into a secure area.
SHOULDER SURFING - When an attacker looks over someone else's shoulder to steal information.
DUMPSTER DIVING - When the attacker searches through the trash for confidential information.



PROTECTION AGAINST SOCIAL ENGINEERING

1. Always destroy confidential information according to the organization policy
2. Always lock or sign out the individual's computer when leaving it unattended.
3. Always report suspicious individuals.
4. Never re-use work related passwords.
5. Never release work related information on social media.
6. Never open emails from untrusted sources.
7. Never give credentials such as password and username to anyone.
8. Never leave your username or password where they can easily be found.




TIPS TO REMEMBER

Slow down.
Research the facts.
Do not let a link be in control of where you land.
Email hijacking is rampant.
Beware of any download.
Foreign offers are fake.

WAYS TO PROTECT YOURSELF

Delete any request for financial information or passwords.
Reject requests for help or offers of help.
Set your spam filters too high.
Secure your computing devices.
